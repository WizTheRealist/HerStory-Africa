## Biography

Pumla Gobodo-Madikizela was born in 1955. She trained as a psychologist and became a leading researcher on trauma, memory, and reconciliation. She served on South Africa’s **Truth and Reconciliation Commission** (TRC), where she interviewed perpetrators and survivors of apartheid violence.

Her book **A Human Being Died That Night** (2003) explores her conversations with Eugene de Kock, a former police commander convicted of murder, and the questions of guilt, remorse, and the possibility of repair. She has held professorships in South Africa and the United States and advises on trauma and reconciliation globally.

## Historical Context

The TRC (1996–1998) was set up to document apartheid-era violations and to consider amnesty and repair. Gobodo-Madikizela’s work helped shape how South Africans and the world understand perpetrator psychology, victim testimony, and the limits and possibilities of forgiveness.

## What She Fought For

Pumla Gobodo-Madikizela has worked for **truth**, **healing**, and **ethical engagement** with the past. She has studied trauma across contexts — including Rwanda and the Holocaust — and has advocated for approaches that centre survivors while engaging complexity.

## Major Achievements

- Member of the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission
- Author of *A Human Being Died That Night* and other works on trauma and reconciliation
- Research professor; global advisor on trauma and transitional justice
- Multiple awards for scholarship and contribution to peace and healing

## Her Impact Today

Pumla Gobodo-Madikizela is a leading voice in the psychology of trauma and reconciliation and in the global conversation on how societies deal with violent pasts.
